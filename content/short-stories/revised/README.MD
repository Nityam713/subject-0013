# revised/
*Where raw drafts become stories. Where the AI earns its place.*

---

## WHAT THIS FOLDER IS

`revised/` receives files that are done being raw and not yet ready to be published.

The job here is surgical. Not creative from scratch — *clarifying* what already exists.
The author's impulse, their subject, their original idea: these are canon.
The revision process sharpens them into something a reader can hold.

A story enters `revised/` rough.
It leaves `revised/` shaped — hook lands, plot holds, quote is exact,
ending shifts without resolving, voice is consistent — and moves to `published/`.

---

## THE REVISION BRIEF — READ THIS FIRST

Every file arriving in `revised/` must carry a revision brief at the top,
written by the author before handoff. See `raw/README.MD` for the full brief format.

If the brief is missing, **do not begin revision.**
Return the file to the author for a brief. Revision without a brief
produces a different story, not a better version of this one.

Before touching anything: read the brief completely.
Identify which tasks are requested. Apply only those tasks.
Do not add unrequested changes — they are not improvements, they are replacements.

---

## THE REVISION TASKS

Six operations are available. The brief specifies which apply.

---

### TASK 1 — SHARPEN THE HOOK

The first sentence must be so specific it could not belong to any other story.
If the raw draft opens with summary, description from outside, or generic interiority —
rewrite the opening until it enters mid-motion, mid-thought, mid-feeling.

**Test:** Read the first sentence in isolation. Does it make a promise about
the specific register and interiority of this story? If it could open a different story,
it is not sharp enough.

WRONG opening registers:
- Summary from outside: *"She was a woman who had always struggled with..."*
- Generic emotion: *"He felt anxious as he waited."*
- Scene-setting before character: *"The café was busy that Tuesday morning."*

RIGHT opening register:
- Specific behavior revealing specific interior, mid-motion, no introduction needed.

---

### TASK 2 — BUILD THE PLOT STRUCTURE

The story is a chain of seven sections. Each section causes the next.
If any section is missing, the chain breaks and the story loses its weight.

The chain:
```
1. HOOK          — mid-motion opening; specific interior; the promise
2. ORDINARY WORLD — subject's life, real and unhurried; stakes established in behavior
3. 0013 ENTERS   — a quality before a person; no announcement; no theatrics
4. THE QUOTE     — quiet, specific, exact; derived from the tell in Section 2
5. THE EXCHANGE  — optional; only if subject's response reveals something new
6. HE LEAVES     — unremarkably; the absence is louder than the exit
7. AFTERMATH     — small, specific shift; theme transformed; no resolution
```

When adding missing sections:
- Match the existing prose's register exactly
- The added sections must be indistinguishable from the author's voice
- Do not rebuild what exists — only add what is absent
- Section 2 must be at least half the story's total length

**The chain test:** Remove any section mentally. If the story still works without it,
that section is not doing its job. Every section must be load-bearing.

---

### TASK 3 — WRITE OR SHARPEN 0013'S QUOTE

This is the most technically demanding task. The quote is the climax.
Everything in the story builds toward it. If it is vague, the story fails.

The quote must satisfy all four conditions:

**1. Derived from the specific tell.**
The tell is the behavioral tic or pattern established in Section 2.
The quote must be traceable back to that exact tell — not a general observation,
but the specific conclusion that only this tell, in this context, produces.

Wrong: *"You're afraid of being left."*
(This is a guess. Any perceptive person could say this.)

Right: *"You've ordered a second coffee you haven't touched. The first one went cold
the same way. Which means you're not here for the coffee — you're here because
leaving would mean admitting the conversation you're waiting for isn't coming."*
(This is a reading. It is derived from specific observed behavior.)

**2. Quiet.**
No pause for effect. No watching for reaction. He states and continues.
The weight lands after he has already moved on.
If the quote has theatrical staging around it, strip it.

**3. Clinical register.**
Interested, not invested. Accurate, not cruel.
He is not trying to hurt them. He is simply stating what is true.
The horror is in the accuracy, not in any intent behind it.

**4. Irremovable from this story.**
Could this quote appear in a different story about a different subject?
If yes — make it more specific until it could not.

**Test the quote:** Read it aloud. Does it land like a diagnosis or a performance?
If it sounds like something a villain would say while enjoying being a villain — rewrite it.

---

### TASK 4 — FIX THE ENDING

The ending is the second most common failure point after the quote.
Raw drafts almost always resolve when they should shift.

**What resolution looks like (wrong):**
- Subject has a realization or revelation
- Subject decides something, understands something, accepts something
- Emotional closure: *"She finally felt ready to..."*
- Moral lesson: *"He understood now that he had been..."*
- Forward motion: *"She knew what she had to do."*

**What a shift looks like (right):**
- A specific small fact about what is different — stated, not interpreted
- The subject is changed but does not know what to do with the change
- The story ends before they figure it out
- The theme from Section 2 reappears in a different form — not resolved, transformed
- The reader supplies the meaning; the story does not

**The theme test for the ending:**
Identify the theme present in Section 2. Find where it appears in Section 7.
It must appear differently — the encounter has changed the subject's relationship to it.
If the theme is absent from the ending, the story has not completed its arc.

**The resolution test:**
Read the last paragraph. Does the subject understand something new?
If yes — pull it back. Make the understanding smaller, less resolved, less certain.
The story ends on the fact of the shift, not the meaning of it.

---

### TASK 5 — ENFORCE BIBLE VOICE AND RULES

Read `bible/writing-rules.md` completely before applying this task.
Every revised story must pass all sixteen rules.

Key failure modes to check:

**0013 is theatrical (Rule 16 — Hannibal test)**
Signs: meaningful pauses, watching for reactions, enjoying his own accuracy,
lines that could be delivered with a smile.
Fix: strip the staging; make his lines shorter and more clinical.

**Interiority is generic (Rules 02, 13)**
Signs: *she felt anxious / he was sad / she didn't know what to think*
Fix: replace every generic emotional summary with a specific behavior or thought.
Not "she felt exposed" — the exact behavior of someone feeling exposed.

**Setting is backdrop (Rule 14)**
Signs: location mentioned once and never used again; no relationship to character interior.
Fix: find what the setting should be doing (mirroring, contrasting, pressuring)
and make it legible in at least one specific detail.

**The world is strange instead of him (Rule 01)**
Signs: uncanny atmosphere, strange coincidences treated as confirmed supernatural.
Fix: every uncanny moment must have a rational explanation alongside it.
The reader decides which is true. The prose does not decide for them.

**The ending resolves (Rule 16 — redemption arc test)**
Already covered in Task 4. Check again here.

---

### TASK 6 — FIX UNCLEAR OR INCOHERENT SECTIONS

Raw drafts written fast often have sections that made sense in the moment
and do not hold together on re-reading.

Rules for this task:
- **Preserve the impulse, fix the execution.** Ask what the section was trying to do
  before deciding how to fix it. The brief's "what this story is trying to be" is the anchor.
- **Do not cut what is merely rough.** Rough is not wrong.
  Only cut what actively contradicts the story's logic or the bible.
- **Do not add new ideas.** Fix what exists.
  The author's ideas are the story. Your ideas are interference
  unless the brief specifically asks for them.

## THE AWARENESS LEVELS

The subject's awareness level determines how the prose behaves.
Check the brief and apply the correct register throughout.

**Level 0 — Unaware**
The subject has no meta-awareness. The story is their life, interrupted.
They do not suspect they are being read by something larger than a strange man.
Prose is grounded, realist, entirely inside their ordinary interiority.

**Level 1 — Sensing**
The subject feels something is wrong about the encounter but cannot name it.
The prose carries a faint dissonance — things are slightly too precise,
the coincidences are slightly too apt. The subject notices but does not conclude.
They leave the story haunted by something they cannot file.

**Level 2 — Knowing**
The subject is a reader themselves. They recognize 0013 not by name
but by what he is — something that exists at the level of story, not person.
The prose can carry more weight. The subject knows they are inside something.
They do not know how to exit it. They may not want to.

**Level 3 — Addressing**
Used once in the entire collection, maximum.
The subject speaks outward — past 0013, past the story, toward the reader.
The prose breaks toward the fourth wall without breaking through it.
Handle with extreme care. This is the story that mirrors the novel's final chapter.

## THE PRESENCE TIERS

**Direct** — 0013 enters the scene. The encounter happens on the page.
Full plot structure applies. His quote must be exact.

**Peripheral** — 0013 is at the edge. Mentioned, glimpsed, felt.
He may have a single line or none. His influence structures the scene
without him entering it. The subject is responding to his proximity, not his presence.

**Absent** — 0013 never appears. Pure aftermath.
The subject is living inside the permanent shift of a past encounter.
He is only present as a gap — something that happened before the story began.
This tier requires the most precise subject interiority. There is nothing else carrying the weight.

**Voiced** — His register bleeds into the prose without announcement.
The subject is telling their story. Somewhere in the middle, something changes.
The register shifts subtly — more precise, more cold, more interested.
The transition must be invisible on first read. On second read, unmissable.

---

## FORMAT TYPES

**Fragment** — A moment. No full arc required. Can be one section of the plot structure
with everything else implied. Ends mid-motion if necessary.

**Document** — Institutional register. Report, case file, transcript, intake form.
Information delivered in passive voice with strategic redactions.
The horror is in what the document cannot see about itself.

**Scenario** — A contained event with stakes beyond the encounter.
Something happens — a decision, a crisis, a situation — and 0013 is present inside it.
The plot structure is present but the world is larger.

**Confession** — First person, present or past tense, the subject addressing someone.
Not 0013. Not the reader. Someone in their life they cannot say this to out loud.
0013's presence is felt through what they are confessing about.

## BEFORE MOVING TO published/

A story leaves `revised/` only when every box is checked:

```
STORY CRAFT
□ Hook: first sentence is specific enough to belong only to this story
□ Plot: all seven sections present and each earning the next
□ Character: subject's specific burden, tell, and real want are legible
□ Setting: location is doing work — not backdrop
□ Stakes: demonstrated in Section 2 behavior — not stated anywhere
□ Conflict: subject's internal conflict exists before 0013 arrives
□ Climax: 0013's quote is derived from the specific tell; quiet; clinical
□ Dialogue: exchange (if present) reveals something new about the subject only
□ Pacing: Section 2 is at least half the story; aftermath is short
□ Theme: present in Section 2; transformed (not repeated) in Section 7
□ Ending: shifts, does not resolve; no revelation, no closure

BIBLE COMPLIANCE
□ 0013 has no internal POV (unless Voiced tier)
□ 0013 is not theatrical — no meaningful pauses, no performed intelligence
□ Subject's interiority is specific — no generic emotional summaries
□ World is realist — no confirmed supernatural
□ Ending does not resolve — it shifts

FILING
□ Revision brief removed from the top of the file
□ All template instruction blocks removed
□ Draft header updated: status: revised
□ Title decided or marked [untitled] deliberately
□ Subject logged in _index/character-appearances.md if named
□ Themes logged in _index/theme-tracker.md
```

When all boxes are checked: move to `published/`. Do not return to `raw/`.
