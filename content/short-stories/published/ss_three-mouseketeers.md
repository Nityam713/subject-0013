# The Three Mouseketeers

---

---

The third trap is still in the kitchen drawer. He has not thrown it away.

He tells himself this is practical — mice come back, they always come back, the building is old and the walls are thin and there is no permanent solution, only management. This is true. He also knows it is not the reason.

The trap is the kind with a yellow plastic door that snaps shut when the mouse steps on the trigger. He bought a pack of four. He has used three. The fourth sits in the drawer next to the takeout menus and the dead batteries he keeps meaning to throw away, and sometimes when he is looking for something else his hand finds it and he holds it for a moment before putting it back.

He is not sure what he is keeping it for.

---

The first mouse was small. He found it in the trap on a Tuesday morning, alive, nose working frantically against the yellow door. He remembers feeling something that was not quite pity — something more like interest. He carried the trap outside in a plastic bag, walked to the end of the street where the garbage disposal area was, and set it down on the concrete.

He should have opened the door and walked away.

Instead he crouched down and opened it halfway. The mouse pushed against the gap. He closed it. Opened it again. The mouse pushed harder. He closed it again. He did this four or five times — he does not remember exactly how many times, he has never counted in the retelling, not even to himself — watching the mouse's small frantic efforts, the way it kept believing the gap would hold this time, the way it kept being wrong.

He tells himself he was playing. He tells himself it was harmless. He tells himself the mouse got free in the end, which is true — it bolted across the concrete and into the road and under the wheels of a passing car, and he stood there holding the empty trap and did not move for a moment, and then walked home faster than he had walked there.

He walked home. He put the trap in the bin. He bought three more.

---

The second mouse came four months later. Bigger. Calmer, somehow, in the trap — it sat in the corner and watched him with small dark eyes while he carried it to the park.

He had thought about the first mouse. He had thought: I will not do the game this time. I will open the door and step back and let it go.

He opened the door. The mouse did not move immediately. He found himself closing it again — just once, he told himself, just to see — and then opening it, and the mouse ran. He watched it run. The park was open and flat in the afternoon light and the mouse ran straight across the grass the way mice do not usually run, out in the open instead of finding a wall or a hedge or a hole, and he had a feeling — not quite a premonition, more like a recognition — that something was watching.

The bird came from somewhere above and to the left. It was fast. The mouse was not.

He stood with the empty trap and the feeling that he had been watched the whole time. Not by the bird. By something else. He could not have said what.

He walked home. He put the trap in the bin. He bought two more.

---

The third mouse was the best one — large and healthy, moving purposefully inside the trap when he found it. He held the trap up briefly to look at it before he carried it outside, the way a person holds up something they have caught. He carried it outside with both hands, carefully, and he told himself: this time I will do nothing. No game. No open-close. I will simply release it and walk away and that will be the end.

He was at the bottom of the steps outside his building when the trap slipped.

He had not done the game. He had not opened the door halfway. He had not done anything except carry it carefully with both hands and then, for one moment, not carefully enough. The trap hit the ground. The door swung open. The mouse was out before he could react.

He did not see the cat until it was already moving.

It had been sitting against the wall of the building, still as a stone, and he had walked right past it without noticing. The cat took the mouse in its jaws with a single clean motion — no chase, no drama, just the precise application of force — and the mouse made one sound and then did not make any more sounds.

He stood on the steps and watched.

He had not done anything wrong this time. He had tried to do everything right. And he had stood there and watched anyway, the same as the other two times, and the mouse had died the same as the other two times, and the only constant across all three was him.

He went inside. He did not put the last trap in the bin.

---

He is sitting on a bench outside the building three days later, not doing anything in particular, when the man sits down at the other end.

He does not notice him at first — there is nothing to notice. A man sitting on a bench. Still in the way that some people are still, not restless, not performing patience, simply present in the way a piece of furniture is present. He is reading something, or appears to be.

After a while the man says, without looking up: "You've been turning something over in your hands for the last ten minutes."

He looks down. He is holding the fourth trap. He does not remember taking it out of his pocket. He does not remember putting it in his pocket.

"I wasn't—" he starts.

"You were there for all three," the man says. Still not looking up. His voice is the same temperature as the air. "You tell yourself the first one got free. You tell yourself the second one was the bird's fault. You tell yourself the third one was an accident." He turns a page. "But you stayed to watch. Every time. You could have looked away. You didn't."

He opens his mouth. He closes it.

The man is already reading again.

---

He sits there for a while after the man leaves — he is not sure when the man left, only that at some point he looked up and the bench was empty and the street was ordinary and nothing had changed except that the trap in his hands felt different now, heavier in a way that had nothing to do with weight.

He thinks about the first mouse pushing against the door. He thinks about the second one running straight across the open grass. He thinks about the cat sitting against the wall, still as a stone, and himself walking right past it without seeing it.

He had been there for all three.

He had not looked away once.

He sits with the trap in his hands for a long time. He does not open it. He does not put it down. He is not sure, later, how long he sat there — he has never counted in the retelling, not even to himself.

The fourth trap is still in his hands when he finally goes inside.

He does not put it back in the drawer.

He does not throw it away either.
